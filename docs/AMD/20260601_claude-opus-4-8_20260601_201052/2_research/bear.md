## The Nvidia H100 Ramp Comparison — The Bull's Most Impressive Argument and Why It Breaks Down Precisely Where It Matters

The bull's comparison to Nvidia's H100 ramp is genuinely his strongest point in this debate, and I want to engage with it at its highest level rather than dismissing it.

He's correct: Nvidia went from ~$0.88 quarterly EPS to ~$4.93 over five quarters during the H100 ramp — a 5.6x increase. AMD needs a "mere" 3.9x increase over four quarters. The analogy sounds compelling.

Here's where it breaks down — and I mean *precisely* where, not vaguely:

**The H100 ramp was driven by a product that had no viable alternative for the critical workload it addressed.** Large language model training at scale required H100-class hardware. There was no AMD equivalent. Every dollar of training compute had to go to Nvidia. AMD was absent from that market. Nvidia's pricing power during the H100 cycle was monopolistic — not competitive. That's why their gross margins hit 75%+ during the ramp.

**AMD's MI300X ramp is happening in a market where Nvidia is simultaneously shipping H200, B100, and Blackwell at scale.** AMD wins design wins on *cost-per-unit-of-compute* against Nvidia's premium-priced products — not because there's no alternative. AMD is the alternative. Nvidia is the default. That competitive dynamic caps AMD's pricing power in a way that simply didn't apply to Nvidia during the H100 era.

So what does AMD's ramp actually look like relative to Nvidia's monopoly ramp? **Lower peak margins. Slower pricing acceleration. Volume growth that depends on AMD winning new design competitions in each successive product cycle rather than filling a vacuum.**

The bull's 3.9x EPS ramp is modeled on Nvidia's monopoly conditions applied to AMD's competitive conditions. That's not conservative. That's category error.
