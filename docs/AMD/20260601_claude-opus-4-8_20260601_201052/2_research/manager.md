**Rationale**: Okay, let me walk you through how I'm reading this one, because both analysts brought real firepower and I don't want to pretend it was a blowout in either direction.

The bull's core case is genuinely strong on the fundamentals that are already in the books: 37.8% YoY revenue growth, record $2.96B quarterly operating cash flow, $8.5B net cash, an 87.5% beat rate over the last eight quarters, and a credible MI350/MI400 product cycle mid-ramp with real hyperscaler design wins at Microsoft, Google, and Meta. He also won a couple of clean exchanges outright — the forward-EPS methodology correction was legitimate (it's a rolling 12-month average of ~$3.27/quarter, not $4.08 across three quarters), the inventory turnover point reframed the bear's "inventory bomb" as demand-linked rather than speculative, and the operating-leverage-on-R&D correction was fair. The Nvidia H100 ramp analogy is the bull's best forward-looking argument: a 3.9x EPS ramp has a real precedent.

But here's where the bear did damage that the bull never fully repaired, and it's the reason I can't get to a Buy. When the bear ran the full margin model using the bull's OWN corrected assumptions — 57% gross margin ceiling, 20% R&D, realistic SG&A and Xilinx amortization — he landed at roughly $7.15 EPS at a $50B revenue base, not $13.08. To actually hit $13.08 you need either $80-90B in revenue (another ~100%+ growth) or Nvidia-level 65%+ margins, which the bull himself conceded AMD structurally can't reach as the price-competitive alternative rather than the monopoly supplier. That is the crux: the entire 39x forward P/E rests on an EPS number that even a charitable model struggles to reach near-term. The bear also landed real blows on the structural competitive picture — Nvidia simultaneously scaling Blackwell, the RTX Spark architectural push, and crucially the hyperscalers' own custom silicon (Google TPU, Microsoft Maia, Amazon Trainium) shrinking AMD's addressable share of the very CapEx ocean the bull is counting on.

The H100 analogy cuts both ways too. Nvidia's monopoly ramp happened with no viable alternative and 75%+ margins; AMD is ramping as the second source, which caps both pricing power and the slope of the earnings curve. So the ramp is possible but not the base case the valuation assumes.

Where I come down: this is a genuinely great company with a real, multi-year AI tailwind — I'm not in the camp that AMD "fails." But at $831B market cap, 169x trailing, ~39x forward on a demanding EPS estimate, RSI 76, ~57% above the 50-day SMA, beta 2.40 and a $26 daily ATR, the risk/reward for NEW capital is roughly symmetric-to-slightly-favorable at best (the bull's own corrected expected value framework lands around $630-650, ~22-26% upside) against a real 25-40% gap-down scenario on any single disappointing print. That asymmetry doesn't justify chasing here, but the demonstrated execution, fortress balance sheet, and intact secular cycle don't justify actively selling a quality compounder into strength either. When the upside case requires near-flawless execution to clear the valuation and the downside requires only "modest disappointment" to hurt badly, but the underlying franchise keeps beating — that is the textbook definition of a balanced book. Maintain the position, don't add at these levels, and let the next one or two earnings prints resolve whether the EPS ramp is real before committing fresh capital.
