**Recommendation**: Overweight

**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through how I'm reading this one, because both analysts brought their A-game and I don't want to pretend it was a blowout in either direction.

The bull's core thesis is genuinely strong and, importantly, it's grounded in things both sides actually agree on: ASML has an unchallenged EUV monopoly that nation-states with unlimited budgets haven't cracked, the AI supercycle is real and multi-year, the financials are exceptional (53% gross margins, $11B FCF, 15.5% revenue CAGR), and there's a $19.4B committed backlog providing real revenue visibility. The bull also landed clean punches on a few specific bear points — the Q1-vs-Q4 "deceleration" framing was correctly exposed as seasonal noise (Q1 is up ~13% YoY on a like-for-like basis), and the customer concentration argument is genuinely blunted by the fact that a monopoly means troubled customers defer rather than defect. TSMC's global fab buildout, Samsung's competitive spending, and even a struggling Intel's existential EUV commitment all support a revenue floor.

The bear, to his enormous credit, never argued the business was bad — he conceded the moat, the technology, and the supercycle outright. His entire case narrowed to one claim: the price is wrong *right now* for new money. And he made that case more rigorously than most bears manage. His best points were: (1) the DCF math — where even on the bull's own preferred 9% WACC, you get a fair value of roughly $550-580B against a $627B market cap, meaning a single-digit-to-low-teens overvaluation rather than a bargain; (2) the guidance gap, where ASML's own 2026 guidance midpoint implies roughly flat revenue while consensus EPS of $47.86 requires a guidance demolition plus margin expansion; and (3) — the single most underrated point in the whole debate — the October 2024 precedent, where ASML fell ~16% in a single session on a below-consensus order-intake quarter. That's not theory. That's the empirical answer to the bull's claim that "sophisticated investors treat timing slips as non-events." The bull never addressed it across multiple rounds, and that silence is telling.

Here's where the bear overreached, though. His probability-weighted return calculation — the centerpiece that supposedly proved Treasuries beat ASML — relied on a 40% probability of a 19.4% drawdown to $1,300 and an artificially capped $1,900 upside. The 40% downside figure is hard to defend against a stock that's above its 10 EMA, 50 SMA, and 200 SMA with RSI at 59.5 (not overbought) and a still-rising MACD line. And capping the upside at $1,900 while the UBS target translates closer to $2,000+ in USD stacks the deck. When you use more honest inputs, the expected value comfortably clears the risk-free rate. So the "Treasuries win" conclusion doesn't hold — but that doesn't mean the bear's underlying caution is wrong.

So where does that leave me? The bull wins the direction — this is a structurally advantaged compounder you want to own, and "wait for a 19% pullback that may never come" is a market-timing bet that has burned people on quality names repeatedly. But the bear wins the entry-discipline argument more than the bull wants to admit. At 54x TTM, 1.3% current FCF yield, a flat guidance midpoint, near-term High-NA timing risk concentrated in a troubled Intel, and that very real October 2024 precedent for sharp single-quarter repricing, this is NOT a back-up-the-truck Buy. The risk is asymmetric enough in the near term that going full Buy and ignoring the drawdown scenario would be the kind of complacency the bear correctly flagged. Overweight is the honest call: constructive conviction in the long thesis, with explicit acknowledgment that the entry premium and event risk warrant building the position deliberately rather than all at once.

Do not get shaken out by histogram contraction or normal volatility (ATR ~$61/day).

Be explicitly prepared to ride through a 15-20% drawdown — treat such a move as an opportunity to complete the position at improved valuation rather than a thesis break.
