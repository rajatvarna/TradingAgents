**Investment Thesis**: The debate converged far more than its volume suggested: every analyst — aggressive, conservative, and neutral — agreed AVGO is an exceptional compounder. The fundamentals are not in dispute: ~29.5% YoY revenue growth at $19B quarterly scale, ~33% FCF growth to ~$8B/quarter, a forward P/E of 24.8x with a sub-1.0 PEG, and a confirmed AI infrastructure tailwind validated by NVIDIA's +74% compute print and Alphabet's $80B capex commitment. The $12.86B deferred-revenue base provides genuine revenue-floor visibility, and pre-earnings upgrades from Susquehanna ($490) and UBS plus Q1 hedge-fund accumulation evidence institutional, not retail, sponsorship. This is the bull's strongest ground and it is durable.

The bear, however, won several important tactical concessions. Most decisively, the aggressive analyst was forced to correct the headline risk-reward from 3:1 to roughly 1.7:1 once the stop at $415 could no longer serve simultaneously as both a guaranteed downside denominator and a soft reassessment trigger — an internal contradiction the conservative analyst correctly exposed. The neutral analyst sharpened this: the probability of a revenue beat is not the same distribution as the probability of a favorable stock reaction, and with the stock above its upper Bollinger Band, ~27% above its 200-day SMA, carrying a bearish MACD divergence (line falling from 21.24 to 9.96 into new price highs), retail sentiment at 12:1, and IV at peak, expectations are richly embedded. The conservative analyst's most underweighted point — the calendar-certain double-catalyst window (earnings June 3rd, jobs report June 6th) interacting with a 1.44 beta — pushes the realistic combined single-week drawdown toward ~18-19%, not the original 8-10% sizing assumption.

Critically, the bear never argued against owning AVGO — only against buying full size this week at this price; their own preferred entry is the same business ~14% lower. That is an entry-timing call, and entry-timing calls against high-quality compounders in confirmed uptrends have a poor track record (the stock ran from ~$289 to ~$446 in two months). But the aggressive analyst's blended-cost-basis argument for front-loading 40-50% collapsed because it assumed chasing a gap to $490, which the plan's own dual-condition second tranche explicitly forbids. The honest synthesis is the neutral analyst's: deploy a meaningful but gap-tolerant ~30% starter to secure exposure to the secular thesis, sized for the ~19% combined-catalyst tail, and reserve the majority for post-earnings deployment when the information set is dramatically richer. This combination — strong long-term thesis, real near-term entry risk — is precisely an Overweight, not a Buy and not a Hold.
