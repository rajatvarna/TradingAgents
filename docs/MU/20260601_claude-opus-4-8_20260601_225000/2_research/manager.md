The bull spun that as "the opportunity," but the more honest read is that the market is efficiently pricing known estimate uncertainty in a notoriously cyclical industry that swung from $8.69B profit to a $5.83B loss in a single year not long ago.

The bull's own best historical analogy — "TSMC 2013" — actually cut against them, since that was a $75B entry, not a $1.17T one.

So why Hold rather than Buy or Sell? Because the two sides are arguing different, both-valid things. The bull is right about the business and the long-term structural thesis. The bear is right about the entry price and near-term risk-reward. The most damning unanswered question in the entire debate was the bear's: "At what price is MU not a buy?" — the bull never answered it, which revealed the BUY rating was more momentum-justification than disciplined valuation. But the bear's AVOID/SELL is equally uncommitted in practice — they explicitly recommended HOLD-with-stops for existing holders and conceded the thesis is real. With a powerful confirmed earnings engine on one side and a stretched, binary-event-laden entry on the other, the evidence is genuinely balanced. That's exactly the situation Hold is meant for: maintain existing exposure, don't chase new money in ahead of a binary catalyst at all-time-high technical extension, and let June 24 resolve the uncertainty.
