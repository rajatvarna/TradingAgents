**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through how I'm reading this debate, because both sides fought hard and each landed real punches.

The bull's core case is genuinely strong and, importantly, it rests on structural facts that nobody disputed: operating income up 18.2% YoY with zero one-time items, a 32.3% operating margin that is best-in-class for media, revenue growth of 16.3%, net debt collapsing from $9.2B to $2.1B, and $22.7B in buybacks. These are durable, not cyclical. The bull's strongest moment was forcing the distinction between the operating business (excellent, clean, growing) and the headline net income (inflated by the interest income windfall). Even after you strip the anomaly, the underlying engine is performing exceptionally.

The bear, to their credit, did the most valuable work in the entire debate: they refused to let the $2.85B non-operating interest income get smuggled into forward earnings power, and they were right to flag that the source is unidentified and the recurrence unconfirmed. That's a legitimate analytical caveat that every buyer is taking on today. The bear also correctly identified near-term headwinds that are real and calendar-driven — the World Cup softening Q2 engagement, European content reinvestment mandates, and a confirmed medium-term technical downtrend (price below a declining 50 SMA, RSI sub-50, negative MACD). And the beta of 1.55 point is fair: in a headline-driven macro tape with live Iran tensions, NFLX will amplify shocks in both directions.

But here's where the bear's case ultimately undercut itself. By the final rounds, the bear conceded — in their own words — that this is "not a broken business," that it's "a fundamentally excellent business," that 27-28x normalized earnings is "a reasonable" to "fair price for a good business," and that the bull thesis "may well prove correct over 18-24 months." When your sell thesis reduces to "great business, fair price, probably higher in two years, but I'd like a better entry," that is not a sell argument — it's a timing and entry-discipline argument. The bull caught this correctly.

On the central valuation fight, the truth landed between the two combatants. The bull's 22.4x forward P/E relied on trusting a consensus EPS that may not be cleanly normalized; the bear's 33-39x relied on double-subtracting the recurring interest income from a single historical quarter and annualizing it. When you do the work properly — normalize away only the one-time excess, recognize the cash hoard genuinely produces some ongoing yield, and use a conservatized forward estimate — you land around 27-28x forward with ~25-29% normalized earnings growth and a PEG near 1.0. That's not screaming cheap, but it's a fair-to-attractive multiple for a business with three independent growth engines (subscription, advertising scaling toward $3B, and consumer products/AI optionality), expanding margins, and a near-net-cash balance sheet.

So where does that leave us? The bear won the point that $86 is not a risk-free, slam-dunk entry — there's an earnings-quality question mark, a soft Q2 catalyst path, and a technical tape that hasn't confirmed a bottom. The bull won the larger point that none of the bear's concerns represent fundamental impairment, and that the structural improvements in this business are real and permanent. The InterPositive/SAG-AFTRA risk is genuine but speculative and equally borne by competitors; it's a reason for caution, not avoidance.

Given a business of this quality at a fair multiple, trading 21-28% below highs, with the bear themselves conceding the long-term direction is likely up — the asymmetry over a 12-month horizon favors accumulating, but the legitimate near-term headwinds and confirmed downtrend argue against backing up the truck at a single price point today. That's why I land on Overweight rather than Buy: constructive conviction in the thesis, paired with respect for the entry-timing risk the bear correctly identified.
