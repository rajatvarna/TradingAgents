**Recommendation**: Overweight

**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through where I landed after sitting with both sides of this NU debate, because it was genuinely one of the closer ones we've run.

The bull's core case is strong and, importantly, the bear conceded most of it: this is a real business, not a 2021-style cash-burning fintech. We're talking 57% YoY revenue growth, ~48% operating margins, ~30% ROE, 135M customers, and $3.16B in annual free cash flow that's up 5x in three years. At a forward P/E around 11x and a PEG under 1.0, you're not paying a premium for that growth. That's the spine of the thesis and it held up under sustained attack.

Now, the bear did real damage in a few places, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. Three points landed:

1. **The tax rate.** The Q1 2026 8.7% effective tax rate clearly flattered headline earnings versus the ~30% normalized level. The bull's own arithmetic showed the forward EPS impact is modest (~$0.04, taking forward P/E from ~11.2x to ~11.7x), but the bear's deeper point stands — we don't have full visibility into how analysts modeled tax normalization across 2026-2027, and that's a genuine source of uncertainty in the "G" underpinning the valuation case.

2. **Compound execution risk and timing.** The bear's strongest contribution was reframing this as a timing question, not a quality question. Q2 2026 earnings in ~6-8 weeks is a real binary catalyst — first post-CFO report, first clean look at normalized taxes, first quantifiable read on Mexico traction. The 0.8^4 ≈ 41% joint-success framing on four concurrent risks is a fair caution.

3. **The BofA/UBS target cuts.** Both houses reviewed Q1 and trimmed targets to ~$16 while staying Neutral. The bull is right that a $16 target on a $13 stock implies ~22% upside and that genuinely bearish analysts don't publish 22%-upside targets. But the bear is also right that the *direction* of revision is down, and anchoring matters.

Where the debate ultimately tipped for me: the bear never actually disputed that expected value is positive. His honest reworking of the bull's probability table still produced ~$14.55 — roughly 11% upside over 18 months. His entire AVOID rests on "positive but insufficient" risk-adjusted return plus a timing preference to wait for Q2 confirmation. That's a respectable argument, but it's an argument for not being maximally aggressive — not an argument that the stock should be sold or avoided. When a skeptic's own honest model lands on positive expected value above forward index returns, on a profitable, cash-generative, structurally advantaged compounder trading below 1.0 PEG, the weight of evidence favors building exposure — just not all at once.

So I'm not going to full Buy, because the bear earned the tax-normalization and Q2-binary concerns and the technical trend genuinely isn't confirmed yet (price below a declining 50 SMA, MACD line still negative). But I'm also not sitting at Hold, because the fundamental asymmetry the bull laid out is real and the bear's case reduces to a timing/sizing argument rather than a thesis-breaker. Overweight is the honest reading: lean in, but stage it and respect the Q2 catalyst.
