## 💣 "Project Finance Logic" Is Doing Enormous Rhetorical Work on a Broken Foundation

I want to engage this analogy directly, because it reveals exactly where the bull's case becomes wishful thinking.

**Traditional project finance has one defining characteristic: the revenue is contractually fixed, deterministic, and enforceable.** When a toll road operator borrows against traffic projections, those projections are based on existing traffic counts, demographic data, and legally binding government concession agreements. When a power plant borrows against offtake agreements, those contracts specify exact volumes at exact prices over exact timeframes.

Oracle's "$100B+ contracted backlog" is not that. It is **Remaining Performance Obligations (RPO)** — an accounting construct that represents the *maximum committed spend* from customers over a *multi-year period* at *negotiated but not always fixed* pricing. Ask any enterprise software analyst what percentage of RPO converts at the top end versus what gets renegotiated, repriced, or delayed, and you will get a very different picture than "$100B locked in."

The bull is treating RPO like it's a toll road concession. **It isn't.** It's a best-case upper bound on future revenue from customers who have committed to *some* level of spend but retain meaningful flexibility on timing and scope. That distinction matters enormously when you're justifying $153 billion in debt against it.
