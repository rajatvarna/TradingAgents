## 🔬 The CapEx Argument: A Real Data Point With the Wrong Interpretation

I want to engage seriously with the bull's strongest technical rebuttal: CapEx fell from $45M to $10.5M while AI workloads accelerated. He says this disproves my gross margin pressure thesis.

Here's the issue: **CapEx and cloud infrastructure costs are different line items.** Snowflake's cloud infrastructure costs — the primary input to Cost of Revenue — are mostly *operating expenses* (compute costs to hyperscalers, support costs, data center fees), not capital expenditures. CapEx represents Snowflake's *own* property, equipment, and leasehold improvements.

The 77% CapEx reduction tells you Snowflake is spending less on its own physical infrastructure — which makes sense for a cloud-native SaaS company that leverages hyperscaler infrastructure. It does *not* tell you anything about the operating cost trajectory of delivering AI compute workloads, which flows through Cost of Revenue and directly affects gross margin.

Gross margin being stable at 66-68% despite accelerating AI workload consumption is interesting, but the CapEx decline cannot be used as evidence of improving AI workload delivery economics. These are orthogonal measures.

The correct lens remains: five consecutive quarters of gross margin stability suggests no improvement in unit economics per dollar of revenue delivered. In a period of supposedly revolutionary AI-driven growth, flat gross margins are not a bull signal. They're evidence that the business model's underlying economics haven't improved even as the growth narrative has intensified.
