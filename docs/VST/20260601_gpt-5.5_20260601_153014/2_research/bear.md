- **Price-to-book:** about **20x**

Exactly. **If.**

That “if” is doing a lot of work.

# 5. The AI-Power Theme Is Real — But `VST` Does Not Own It

I agree that AI and data-center electricity demand are real. But the path from “AI needs power” to “`VST` shareholders earn structurally higher returns” is not automatic.

There are several frictions:

- Data centers may pursue dedicated generation or self-generation.
- Competitors are also targeting the same demand.
- PJM rules may affect interconnection, cost allocation, and capacity treatment.
- Regulators may prioritize grid reliability and consumer affordability over generator upside.
- Hedges can limit upside participation.
- Fuel costs, emissions rules, and capex needs can offset power-price benefits.

`VST` has exposure to the AI-power theme. That is different from owning the theme.

The bull says regulatory complexity may favor incumbents. Maybe. But that is one possible outcome, not a certainty. A restrictive PJM framework could also slow demand conversion, shift costs, or limit economics.

# 8. Moss Landing and Insider Selling Are Not Throwaway Risks

The bull says Moss Landing is known and therefore probably priced in. I disagree.

Known does not mean resolved.

The Moss Landing battery incident can still create:

- Regulatory scrutiny
- Insurance cost pressure
- Litigation risk
- Operational restrictions
- Project delays
- Reputational damage
- Higher future storage costs

Zacks already cited `VST`’s underperformance after the incident. Until the liability and regulatory picture is clearer, investors should not dismiss it as sentiment noise.

Same with insider selling. It is not automatically bearish, but it matters in context. If insiders are disposing of stock while the market is being asked to buy into a major AI-power rerating, that should temper enthusiasm.

And corporate buybacks are not equivalent to insiders buying with personal capital — especially when buybacks exceeded free cash flow in the quarter.
